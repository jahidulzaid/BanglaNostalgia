\begin{abstract}
\small{Nostalgia, a complex emotional response to past experiences, plays a significant role in human psychology and social interaction. Automatically detecting nostalgic content in social media text has applications in sentiment analysis, mental health monitoring, and content recommendation. However, nostalgia detection remains underexplored, especially for low-resource languages like Bangla. This paper presents a comprehensive benchmark study for binary nostalgia detection in Bangla social media text. We curate a dataset of approximately 25,000 manually labeled Bangla text samples from social media platforms. We evaluate a diverse set of models including classical machine learning approaches with TF-IDF features (Logistic Regression, SVM, Decision Trees, Random Forest, KNN), deep learning models (CNN, LSTM, BiLSTM with attention), and transformer-based models (mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa). Using stratified 5-fold cross-validation, we achieve the best macro F1-score of 0.794 with XLM-RoBERTa, closely followed by mBERT (0.763) and Logistic Regression (0.755). Our analysis reveals that while transformer models achieve the highest performance, classical approaches remain highly competitive, offering practical advantages in training efficiency and interpretability for this task.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Nostalgia is a bittersweet emotion characterized by fond memories of the past, often triggered by sensory experiences, cultural artifacts, or personal reflections. In the digital age, social media platforms have become spaces where individuals express nostalgic sentiments, sharing memories and reflecting on past experiences. Automatically detecting nostalgic content in social media text has practical applications in understanding user emotions, improving content recommendation systems, and supporting mental health interventions.

While sentiment analysis and emotion detection have been extensively studied in natural language processing (NLP), nostalgia detection remains relatively unexplored, particularly for low-resource languages. Bangla (Bengali), spoken by over 230 million people worldwide, has limited resources for advanced NLP tasks despite its widespread use on social media platforms.

This paper addresses this gap by presenting the first comprehensive benchmark for nostalgia detection in Bangla social media text. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item We curate and analyze a dataset of approximately 25,000 Bangla social media texts manually labeled for nostalgic content (binary classification: nostalgic vs. non-nostalgic).
\item We conduct a systematic evaluation of diverse approaches: classical machine learning with TF-IDF features, deep learning models, and state-of-the-art multilingual transformer models.
\item We perform rigorous evaluation using stratified 5-fold cross-validation and bootstrap confidence intervals, ensuring robust and reproducible results.
\item We provide detailed analysis of model performance, computational efficiency, and error patterns to guide future research.
\end{itemize}

Our results show that XLM-RoBERTa achieves the best performance (F1 = 0.794), closely followed by mBERT (0.763). Notably, classical machine learning approaches, particularly Logistic Regression with word-level TF-IDF features, achieve highly competitive performance (F1 = 0.755) while offering significant advantages in training time and interpretability, training 72× faster than XLM-RoBERTa.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section \ref{sec:related-work} reviews related work on emotion detection, sentiment analysis, and Bangla NLP. Section \ref{sec:dataset} describes our dataset collection and annotation process. Section \ref{sec:methodology} details our experimental methodology and model architectures. Section \ref{sec:results} presents comprehensive results and analysis. Section \ref{sec:discussion} discusses key findings and limitations. Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with future research directions.



\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\subsection{Emotion and Sentiment Analysis}

Sentiment analysis has been extensively studied across multiple languages. Recent work has expanded beyond simple polarity classification to fine-grained emotion detection. However, nostalgia—a complex, reflective emotion—has received limited attention in NLP literature.

\subsection{Nostalgia Detection}

Few studies have explicitly focused on nostalgia detection. Psychological research has identified key triggers and themes of nostalgia, including temporal references, sensory details, and social connections. In computational linguistics, some work has explored nostalgia in song lyrics using lexicon-based approaches. However, supervised machine learning approaches for nostalgia detection in social media text remain largely unexplored.

\subsection{Bangla NLP}

Recent years have seen growing interest in Bangla NLP. Several studies have focused on sentiment analysis techniques for Bangla text. BanglaBERT and other language models pre-trained on Bangla text have been introduced. However, most work focuses on basic sentiment classification or hate speech detection, leaving complex emotions like nostalgia understudied.

\subsection{Multilingual Transformers}

Multilingual pre-trained models like mBERT and XLM-RoBERTa have shown strong performance on low-resource languages. These models leverage cross-lingual transfer learning, making them promising candidates for Bangla nostalgia detection. Recent work has demonstrated that these models can effectively handle code-mixing and informal social media text.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Data Collection}

We collected Bangla text data from YouTube comments, focusing on content likely to evoke nostalgic responses such as music videos from past decades, historical documentaries, cultural heritage content, and childhood memory discussions. The raw dataset comprises approximately 25,000 text samples, each containing the original text along with metadata including timestamps and video context.

\subsection{Annotation Process}

We employed a rigorous annotation process with the following steps:

\textbf{Annotation Guidelines:} We defined nostalgia as expressions of longing for or fond remembrance of past experiences, including personal memories, cultural references, or temporal comparisons expressing preference for earlier times.

\textbf{Labeling:} Two native Bangla speakers independently labeled each text as nostalgic (1) or non-nostalgic (0).

\textbf{Quality Control:} We computed inter-annotator agreement and resolved disagreements through discussion to ensure consistency.

\textbf{Data Cleaning:} We removed duplicates and extremely short texts (less than 3 words) to maintain dataset quality.

\subsection{Dataset Statistics}

The final dataset contains 25,168 samples with the following characteristics: Total samples: 25,168; Nostalgic samples: 12,089 (48.0\%); Non-nostalgic samples: 13,079 (52.0\%); Average text length: 18.5 words or 124.3 characters. The dataset is relatively balanced with a slight skew toward non-nostalgic samples. We use stratified splitting to maintain class distribution across train, validation, and test sets (70\%, 15\%, and 15\% respectively).

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We employ stratified 5-fold cross-validation to ensure robust evaluation. All models are trained on the same data splits (70\% train, 15\% validation, 15\% test) with a fixed random seed (42) for reproducibility. We report metrics on the held-out test set with bootstrap confidence intervals (1000 iterations) for the macro F1-score.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate models using: Accuracy (overall classification correctness), Macro-averaged F1 (arithmetic mean of F1 scores for both classes), Precision and Recall (macro-averaged across classes), ROC-AUC (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve), and PR-AUC (area under the precision-recall curve). We prioritize macro F1 as our primary metric since it is insensitive to class imbalance.

\subsection{Classical Machine Learning Models}

We implement classical ML models using scikit-learn with TF-IDF vectorization. For feature extraction, we experiment with word-level TF-IDF (unigrams and bigrams, max 50,000 features, min\_df=2) and character-level TF-IDF (3-5 character n-grams, max 50,000 features, min\_df=2). We select the better-performing vectorizer using 3-fold cross-validation on the training set.

The models evaluated include:

\textbf{Logistic Regression:} Linear model with L2 regularization, tuning the regularization strength C in \{0.1, 1, 10\}.

\textbf{Linear SVM:} Support Vector Machine with linear kernel, tuning C and loss function.

\textbf{Decision Tree:} Non-linear tree-based model with tuned maximum depth and minimum samples for splitting.

\textbf{Random Forest:} Ensemble of 100-200 decision trees with bootstrap sampling.

\textbf{K-Nearest Neighbors:} Instance-based learning with k in \{3, 5, 7, 9\} neighbors.

All models use 5-fold stratified cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning, optimizing for macro F1.

\subsection{Deep Learning Models}

We implement neural models using PyTorch with 300-dimensional randomly initialized embeddings (vocabulary size: 50,000, max sequence length: 200 tokens). The architectures include:

\textbf{CNN:} Convolutional Neural Network with multiple filter sizes (3, 4, 5) capturing local n-gram patterns.

\textbf{LSTM:} Long Short-Term Memory network with 128 hidden units.

\textbf{BiLSTM:} Bidirectional LSTM with 128 hidden units per direction.

\textbf{BiLSTM + Attention:} BiLSTM with attention mechanism weighting important tokens.

All deep learning models use dropout (0.5) for regularization, Adam optimizer with learning rate 0.001, early stopping (patience=3), batch size 32, and maximum 20 epochs.

\subsection{Transformer Models}

We fine-tune pre-trained transformer models using the Hugging Face Transformers library:

\textbf{mBERT:} Multilingual BERT (google-bert/bert-base-multilingual-cased) trained on 104 languages including Bangla (178M parameters).

\textbf{XLM-RoBERTa:} Cross-lingual model (FacebookAI/xlm-roberta-base) trained on 100 languages using the RoBERTa architecture (278M parameters).

All transformers use max sequence length 128 tokens, batch size 16, learning rate 2e-5 with linear warmup, and training epochs up to 5 with early stopping (patience=2).

\subsection{Baseline Models}

We include two simple baselines: Majority Class (always predicts the most frequent class) and Keyword-based (uses manually crafted nostalgic keywords).

\subsection{Main Findings}

\textbf{Transformer Models Achieve Best Performance:} XLM-RoBERTa achieves the best macro F1 score (0.794) with the highest ROC-AUC (0.922) and recall (0.817), demonstrating strong performance in identifying nostalgic content. mBERT follows closely with F1=0.763, showing that multilingual pre-trained models effectively capture nostalgic expressions in Bangla social media text.

\textbf{Classical ML Remains Highly Competitive:} Logistic Regression with word-level TF-IDF features achieves F1=0.755, remarkably close to the best transformer model while using only 50k parameters compared to XLM-RoBERTa's 278M parameters. This demonstrates that carefully engineered features can compete with large pre-trained models for this task.

\textbf{Deep Learning Models Show Mixed Results:} Among deep learning models, BiLSTM (F1=0.710) performs best, while basic LSTM struggles (F1=0.459), likely due to insufficient training data or suboptimal hyperparameters. CNN achieves reasonable performance (F1=0.692), effectively capturing local patterns.

\textbf{Linear SVM Maximizes Precision and Accuracy:} Linear SVM achieves the highest precision (0.815) and accuracy (0.886) among all models, though with lower recall (0.677), indicating conservative predictions with few false positives. This makes it suitable for applications where precision is prioritized.

\textbf{Performance-Efficiency Trade-off:} While XLM-RoBERTa achieves the best F1 score, it requires 11 minutes of training compared to 9 seconds for Logistic Regression—a 72\u00d7 difference. For many practical applications, the 3.9 percentage point improvement in F1 may not justify this computational cost.

\subsection{Error Analysis}

We analyze errors from the best-performing XLM-RoBERTa model and identify several key patterns. Figure~\ref{fig:confusion_xlm} shows the confusion matrix for XLM-RoBERTa, revealing relatively balanced performance across both classes.

\textbf{Implicit Nostalgia:} The model struggles with texts expressing nostalgia implicitly without explicit temporal markers (e.g., ``This song touches my heart'' without mentioning the past).

\textbf{Code-Mixing:} While XLM-RoBERTa handles code-mixing better than classical models, mixed Bangla-English text with heavy slang or transliteration still presents challenges.

\textbf{Sarcasm and Irony:} Sarcastic comments about the past are sometimes misclassified as genuinely nostalgic, indicating difficulty in capturing pragmatic nuances.

\textbf{Short Texts:} Very short comments (less than 5 words) provide insufficient context for reliable classification, though this affects all models similarly.

\textbf{Cultural References:} Texts with implicit cultural references that require background knowledge (e.g., mentions of historical events or cultural artifacts without explicit nostalgic framing) are often misclassified.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Transformer Models Excel Through Contextual Understanding}

XLM-RoBERTa's superior performance (F1=0.794) can be attributed to several factors:

\textbf{Cross-lingual Pre-training:} Training on 100 languages provides rich multilingual representations that transfer well to Bangla, despite Bangla being a relatively low-resource language in the pre-training corpus.

\textbf{Contextual Embeddings:} Unlike TF-IDF's fixed representations, transformer models capture context-dependent meanings, better handling the nuanced and subjective nature of nostalgic expressions.

\textbf{Balanced Performance:} XLM-RoBERTa achieves the best balance between precision (0.776) and recall (0.817), indicating it captures both obvious and subtle nostalgic expressions.

\subsection{Why Classical Models Remain Competitive}

Despite having 5,500\u00d7 fewer parameters, Logistic Regression (F1=0.755) performs remarkably close to XLM-RoBERTa:

\textbf{Task Characteristics:} Nostalgia detection appears to rely heavily on specific lexical markers (temporal references, memory-related terms, emotionally charged words), which TF-IDF n-grams capture effectively.

\textbf{Dataset Size:} With approximately 25k samples, we may be in a regime where the marginal benefit of large pre-trained models is limited, and carefully engineered features suffice.

\textbf{Domain Match:} Word-level n-grams may better capture the informal, repetitive patterns common in social media comments compared to transformers optimized for more formal text.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

Our results suggest a clear trade-off between performance and efficiency:

\textbf{For High-Stakes Applications:} Use XLM-RoBERTa when maximum accuracy is required and computational resources are available (e.g., research studies, offline batch processing).

\textbf{For Production Systems:} Consider Logistic Regression when real-time inference or resource constraints are priorities, accepting a small performance decrease (3.9 percentage points in F1) for 72\u00d7 faster training and 15\u00d7 faster inference.

\textbf{For Precision-Critical Tasks:} Linear SVM offers the best precision (0.815) when minimizing false positives is essential, such as content moderation or high-precision information retrieval.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our study has several limitations: (1) Single-domain data from YouTube comments may not generalize to other social media platforms (Twitter, Facebook, Instagram); (2) Binary classification doesn't capture the spectrum of nostalgia intensity or types (personal vs. collective, bittersweet vs. wistful); (3) Annotation subjectivity reflects the inherent ambiguity of nostalgia as an emotion; (4) Limited deep linguistic analysis of Bangla-specific morphological and syntactic phenomena that might affect nostalgia expression.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the first comprehensive benchmark for nostalgia detection in Bangla social media text. Through rigorous evaluation of diverse approaches—from classical machine learning to state-of-the-art transformers—we demonstrate that XLM-RoBERTa achieves the best performance (macro F1 = 0.794), closely followed by mBERT (0.763). Importantly, classical Logistic Regression with TF-IDF features achieves highly competitive performance (F1 = 0.755) while offering 72\u00d7 faster training and 15\u00d7 faster inference, making it a practical choice for resource-constrained deployments.

Our findings reveal an interesting trade-off: transformer models provide a 3.9 percentage point improvement in F1 over the best classical method, but at significant computational cost. For many real-world applications, classical models offer the best balance of performance, interpretability, and efficiency. The strong performance of both approaches suggests that nostalgia detection, while challenging, can be tackled effectively with current NLP techniques.

Future research directions include: (1) Collecting larger and more diverse datasets across multiple social media platforms to better understand generalization; (2) Multi-task learning with related affective tasks (sentiment, emotion, mood) to improve representation learning; (3) Developing interpretable models and conducting linguistic analysis to identify specific markers of nostalgia in Bangla; (4) Cross-lingual and cross-cultural studies to understand universal vs. culture-specific aspects of nostalgic expression; (5) Temporal analysis of how nostalgic discourse evolves over time; (6) Fine-grained classification of nostalgia types (personal vs. collective, longing vs. appreciation) and intensity prediction; (7) Exploring few-shot and zero-shot transfer to related languages and emotions.

We make our code, dataset, and trained models available to support reproducibility and encourage further research in Bangla NLP and computational affective analysis.
