## **Nostalgia Detection in Bangla User-Generated Text:** Summary of a Binary Classification Study

This work addresses **automatic detection of nostalgic content** in Bangla user-generated text, formulating the task as a **binary classification problem** (nostalgic vs. not nostalgic). The study is motivated by broader advances in Bangla text classification for sentiment and emotion analysis, where both classical and transformer-based models have shown strong performance on noisy social media and news data  (Alam et al., 2020; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Sen et al., 2021).

A new dataset of approximately **20,000 Bangla texts** is curated from social media posts, comments, and forums. Each instance is manually annotated as nostalgic or non‑nostalgic, following clear guidelines inspired by prior Bangla emotion and sentiment annotation efforts  (Akter et al., 2024; Rahman & Hanif, 2019). The corpus reflects informal orthography, code-mixing, and diverse topics, aligning with challenges reported for Bangla social media and e‑commerce review data  (Das et al., 2023; Shanto et al., 2023).

For classical baselines, the study trains **Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machines, Decision Tree, and Random Forest** using **TF‑IDF** features, following common practice in Bangla sentiment and document classification  (Ayman et al., 2025; Das et al., 2023; Ahmed et al., 2022; Habibullah et al., 2023). Models are evaluated under **k‑fold cross‑validation**, and mean ± standard deviation are reported for **accuracy, macro‑F1, nostalgic‑class F1, precision, recall, and ROC‑AUC**, consistent with comparative Bangla text classification work  (Das et al., 2023; Hasib et al., 2023; Reusens et al., 2024). These baselines provide competitive performance and serve as efficient, interpretable reference models, especially valuable for low‑resource deployments  (Reusens et al., 2024; Dalaorao, 2025).

The study then fine‑tunes several **transformer-based models**, including multilingual BERT variants and Bangla-specific transformers such as **BanglaBERT/Bangla‑BERT**, on the same dataset and splits  (Kowsher et al., 2022; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Alam et al., 2020). Prior research shows that such models substantially improve Bangla sentiment, news, and general NLU tasks over classical approaches and word‑embedding baselines  (Kowsher et al., 2022; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Hasib et al., 2023; Bhowmik et al., 2022; Alam et al., 2020). In line with this evidence, the nostalgia detection experiments find that transformer models **consistently outperform TF‑IDF + classical classifiers** across all metrics, with the largest gains in macro‑F1, nostalgic‑class F1, and ROC‑AUC, indicating better handling of class imbalance and subtle contextual cues  (Kowsher et al., 2022; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Hasib et al., 2023; Alam et al., 2020).

Analysis highlights that transformers better capture **contextual and figurative expressions of nostalgia**, such as temporally anchored memories and culturally specific references, where bag‑of‑words features are insufficient  (Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Sen et al., 2021; Alam et al., 2020). However, classical models remain **competitive on shorter, lexically explicit posts**, and offer advantages in training speed, resource usage, and interpretability—an important consideration emphasized more broadly in Bangla NLP and general text classification benchmarks  (Sen et al., 2021; Reusens et al., 2024; Dalaorao, 2025).

The experimental methodology is detailed to support **reproducibility**: tokenization and normalization choices tailored to Bangla, stop‑word handling, TF‑IDF parameterization, train–validation–test or cross‑validation protocols, hyperparameter grids, and implementation details for fine‑tuning transformers, following established practices in Bangla NLP and text classification comparisons  (Ayman et al., 2025; Das et al., 2023; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Hasib et al., 2023; Sen et al., 2021; Alam et al., 2020). Evaluation uses standard supervised metrics plus ROC‑AUC and class‑specific F1 to give a robust view of performance in the presence of potential class imbalance  (Ayman et al., 2025; Das et al., 2023; Başarslan & Bal, 2025; Hasib et al., 2023; Reusens et al., 2024).

Beyond nostalgia detection itself, the study contributes a **publicly reusable Bangla nostalgia corpus** and a **strong transformer baseline**, aligning with ongoing efforts to build benchmarks and pretrained models for low‑resource languages  (Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Sen et al., 2021). The findings reinforce that well‑designed transformer models are particularly beneficial for **semantically nuanced affective phenomena** in Bangla, while also underscoring scenarios where lighter classical models remain practically attractive in constrained environments  (Kowsher et al., 2022; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Sen et al., 2021; Reusens et al., 2024; Dalaorao, 2025). Future work directions include multi‑label modeling of nostalgia alongside other emotions, domain adaptation across platforms, interpretability analyses for cultural narratives, and cross‑lingual transfer leveraging related low‑resource languages  (Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Hasib et al., 2023; Sen et al., 2021; Alam et al., 2020).
